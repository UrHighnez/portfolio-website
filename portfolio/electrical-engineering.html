ElectricalEngineering

Prototyping smart objects

When the pandemic pushed our electrical-engineering courses onto Zoom, our professor simply shipped the equipment to every student's home.

So we learned to solder components, design our own circuit board and send it to production — a real lockdown highlight. I built 'Dal Segno', a glove that turns the distance read by an ultrasonic sensor into sound, mapped to piano notes and their lengths.

In a fifth-semester design course with students from KISD (Köln International School of Design), my team built a radio-style prototype that tracks the global death count — a speculative piece inspired by design duo Dunne & Raby.

One of the most fun projects: building remote-controlled vehicles to explore campus. My team went for the hardest option — a flying zeppelin. It never stayed up more than a few seconds (getting the weight right was brutal), but the microcontrollers and the design challenge made it worth it.

A bigger fourth-semester project explored the Internet of Things. With a teammate I designed a smart lock you could open remotely via an app or voice control — no more lost keys, in theory. The prototype stayed impractical, but we learned a lot about machine learning and microcontrollers along the way.
